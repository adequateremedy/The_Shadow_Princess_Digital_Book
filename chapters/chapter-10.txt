The forest carried them for six days.
Six long days of hidden paths and cautious travel.
Six days of listening before speaking.
Six days of watching every road crossing and every distant plume of smoke.
The world had changed.
Not merely politically.
Physically.
The roads themselves felt different.
More patrols.
More wagons.
More industry.
More signs of expansion.
More mortals.
Every mile westward revealed evidence.
Fresh tracks cut through woodland.
New bridges crossing streams.
Construction camps appearing where none had existed before.
Human ambition was spreading across Eidolon.
Relentless.
Hungry.
Organized.
The deeper they traveled, the more visible it became.
By the sixth day, the horizon itself had changed.
Steelmills announced its presence long before anyone saw the city.
The smoke came first.
Dark columns rising into the sky.
Too many to count.
Too large to ignore.
Morrigan noticed them shortly after dawn.
She stared across rolling hills toward the distant haze.
A feeling settled in her stomach.
Unease.
Instinct.
The same sensation prey felt before glimpsing into the eyes of their predator.
Dagda followed her gaze.
Neither spoke.
Because neither needed words.
The smoke said enough.
________________________________________
By afternoon they reached the outskirts.
The city looked nothing like Cogsbin.
Nothing.
Where Cogsbin had felt alive, Steelmills felt driven.
Where Cogsbin had felt warm, Steelmills felt mechanical.
Even the architecture seemed different.
Buildings stood taller.
Narrower.
More functional.
Less beautiful.
Everywhere metal dominated.
Steel railings.
Copper bridges.
Iron towers.
The streets pulsed with movement.
Workers hurried between factories.
Carts transported raw materials.
Industrial whistles echoed constantly.
The sound never seemed to stop.
It pierced the air at regular intervals.
Sharp.
Demanding.
Artificial.
Morrigan hated it immediately.
Not because it was loud.
Because the machines felt hungry.
The entire city felt hungry.
Like a snake consuming its tail in pursuit of growth.
The shadows felt it too.
They remained restless.
Uneasy.
Refusing to settle.
________________________________________
The group entered carefully.
Quietly.
Avoiding attention.
The safe house waiting for them belonged to another Shadow Fae that was able to disguise themselves as mortal, with glamour.
A modest building tucked between warehouses near the northern district.
Unremarkable.
Forgettable.
Exactly what they needed.
The moment they arrived, the door opened, and a woman emerged.
Tall.
Dark-haired.
Akasha-dominant.
Dagda recognized her instantly.
"Nessa."
Relief crossed her face, briefly, then vanished.
The expression troubled him immediately.
Nessa was not relieved because they arrived.
She was relieved because they arrived alive.
The distinction mattered.
The distinction frightened him.
________________________________________
The safe house remained silent until every door was locked.
Every window covered.
Every traveler accounted for.
Only then did conversation begin.
Nessa wasted no time.
"You're too late."
The words struck the room like a physical blow.
Dagda frowned.
"What happened?"
Nessa looked exhausted.
The kind of exhaustion that accumulated over months.
Not days.
Months.
"Everything."
Silence followed.
She moved toward a table covered in maps.
Documents.
Notes.
Evidence.
So much evidence.
Too much.
The sight alone answered half the question.
Nessa pointed toward several marked locations.
Facilities.
Holding sites.
Processing centers.
The symbols covered nearly every district of the city.
Morrigan stared.
Disbelief slowly giving way to horror.
"There are that many?"
Nessa laughed bitterly.
"No."
A pause.
"Those are only the ones we found."
The room grew quiet.
Very quiet.
Because everyone understood what that meant.
If those were only the known locations...
How many remained hidden?
________________________________________
The truth emerged piece by piece.
Patiently.
Painfully.
Steelmills had become the center of everything.
The machines.
The extraction.
The refinement.
The industries.
The Corporations.
All roads seemed to lead here.
The city had embraced steam completely.
Celebrated it.
Built itself around it.
The lie no longer hid in shadows.
It stood openly in the streets.
Factories operated day and night.
Workers proudly discussed production quotas.
Newspapers praised innovation.
Investors grew wealthy.
The public applauded.
And beneath it all—
Fae died.
Every day.
Quietly.
Efficiently.
Systematically.
Morrigan listened without interruption.
Her daughter shifted within her.
A small movement.
A reminder.
A reason.
A future.
Nessa's voice remained steady.
Professional.
Detached.
Yet beneath it, grief lingered, raw and unresolved.
"Most don't…..most don’t even know."
The statement surprised several listeners.
Nessa noticed immediately.
"They know… something."
Her expression hardened.
"They don't know… everything."
Dagda nodded slowly.
Because it held truth.
The machinery remained visible.
The source remained hidden.
Many mortals probably suspected nothing.
Others likely suspected enough to avoid questions.
The difference hardly mattered to the dead.
Still… truth mattered… even when painful.
________________________________________
Three days later Dagda saw the first Corporation himself.
That proved more than sufficient.
The opportunity arrived through information gathered by local allies.
A transport route.
Predictable timing.
Limited security.
The sort of weakness investigators searched for.
He left before dawn.
Alone.
The decision frustrated Morrigan.
The argument lasted nearly twenty minutes.
Long by their standards.
Neither enjoyed fighting.
Neither enjoyed disagreement.
Yet neither yielded easily when conviction entered the discussion.
"I should go."
Morrigan folded her arms.
"No."
Dagda sighed.
"Morrigan."
"No."
His expression shifted, half exasperation, half affection, but entirely familiar.
"You are not joining a reconnaissance mission."
"I'm perfectly capable."
"I know."
"Then—"
"You are carrying my child."
The words interrupted her instantly.
Silence followed.
Morrigan glared.
Dagda remained stubborn.
Neither moved.
Finally, Morrigan pointed toward him.
"I dislike you occasionally."
A faint smile appeared.
"Only occasionally?"
The answer almost earned a laugh… almost.
Instead, she rolled her eyes dramatically.
The argument ended exactly where it began.
Dagda leaving alone.
Both of them unhappy about it.
________________________________________
The Corporate facility stood beyond the eastern industrial district.
Hidden behind warehouses.
Protected by walls.
Guarded by armed, mortal personnel.
Everything appeared ordinary at first glance.
That was the most disturbing part; normalcy… routine.
Workers arrived and departed.
Deliveries entered through large gates.
Smoke drifted from chimneys.
Industry.
Productivity.
Progress.
The lie wore familiar clothing.
Dagda watched from concealment.
Patient.
Still.
The shadows helped.
Wrapping him in obscurity.
Listening.
Observing.
Hours passed.
Then the wagon arrived.
That moment changed everything.
It appeared unremarkable and covered.
It was obviously guarded, but still… ordinary.
Until the rear canvas shifted.
Just slightly.
A glimpse.
Nothing more.
Yet enough.
A hand.
Thin and bound.
Fae…
Dagda felt sick.
The wagon continued forward.
Passing through gates.
Disappearing inside.
Gone.
The image remained.
Burned into memory.
A hand reaching briefly toward daylight.
Then swallowed by gates and walls.
________________________________________
He remained until sunset.
Gathering information.
Confirming suspicions.
Watching.
Learning.
Every discovery made the situation worse.
The facility processed living Fae.
That much became undeniable.
The wagon had proved it.
The guards had proved it.
The security measures had proved it.
No such industrial innovation required such precautions.
No legitimate operation had ever before required cages.
The evidence existed everywhere.
Hidden in plain sight.
Protected by the authority of a mortal government.
Protected by human money.
Protected by silence.
Dagda returned after dark.
His expression told the story before he spoke.
Nessa understood immediately.
Morrigan did too.
No one interrupted or rushed him.
The room waited.
Dagda sat heavily.
Then spoke quietly and carefully.
The details emerged.
Each one sharpening the horror.
Each one confirming fears.
By the time he finished, no one remained uncertain.
The disappearances.
The machines.
The laws.
The propaganda.
All of it connected.
All of it was a part of the same corporation.
A Corporation run by a machine.
A machine larger than any factory.
Larger than any city.
A machine built from mortal greed.
________________________________________
That night sleep proved impossible for everyone.
Everyone in the safe house remained awake long after midnight.
Maps covered tables.
Strategies emerged.
Arguments followed.
Possibilities discussed.
Yet every path seemed insufficient.
The enemy possessed resources, numbers, infrastructure, and authority.
The Shadow Fae possessed… shadows.
They held onto their determination.
And increasingly desperate hope.
The imbalance felt obvious.
The imbalance felt terrifying.
Morrigan sat near a second-story window overlooking the city of Steelmills, while it glowed
beneath darkness.
Factory fires painted the horizon orange.
Smoke from refined Fae Essence blurred the stars, as it burned.
The sight felt unnatural.
Like a wound spreading across the landscape.
Dagda joined her.
Silent as always.
Comfortable as always.
Together they watched the city, until eventually Morrigan whispered—
"We can't stay here."
Dagda nodded.
"No."
The answer came immediately.
Neither needed debate.
Neither needed discussion.
The conclusion had already formed.
Steelmills was lost.
The city had sunk too deeply into the lie.
The Corporation itself had rooted too firmly.
The corruption had spread too widely.
The sanctuary they sought did not exist here.
Morrigan rested a hand upon her stomach.
Their child moved again.
Gentle.
Alive.
The sensation brought an unexpected calm, not safety; but purpose.
A reason to continue… to survive.
Beyond Steelmills lay Crudsder.
The final city.
The final refuge.
The great wooded city of hope.
The gathering place already whispered about fleeing Fae, finding their way to the edges of its forested outer rim.
Hope remained there.
At least for now.
Yet even as Morrigan looked westward through the darkness, she felt something else.
A pressure…or maybe a pull.
It was subtle and ancient.
Nearly impossible to describe.
The shadows felt it too.
They shifted uneasily around the room.
Listening and waiting.
It was as though the world itself had begun holding its breath.
As though somewhere ahead, hidden beyond forests and fear and destiny, a door was preparing to open.
A door no one yet knew existed.
A door that would change everything… forever.
