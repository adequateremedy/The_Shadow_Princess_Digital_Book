The rain ended.
For nearly two weeks, not a single drop fell upon Cogsbin.
The skies remained clear.
Blue stretched endlessly above the city.
Airships drifted across the horizon like floating islands.
Markets overflowed with merchants.
Musicians played in public squares.
Children laughed in alleyways.
Life appeared unchanged.
That was what made it frightening.
The world often looked its most beautiful immediately before disaster.
Morrigan noticed it while standing outside the bakery one morning.
A young human girl sat upon the curb sharing a sweet roll with a Jala Fae boy.
Neither could have been older than eight.
The boy accidentally dropped half the pastry.
The girl laughed and split hers in half without hesitation.
The simple kindness should have warmed her.
Instead it hurt.
Because she knew something they did not.
Because innocence never understood how close it stood to loss.
Dagda stepped beside her.
He followed her gaze.
For a long moment neither spoke.
Then Morrigan sighed.
"They deserve better."
Dagda's eyes remained on the children.
"Yes."
Nothing more.
Nothing needed.
The truth existed plainly enough.
They deserved better than fear.
Better than greed.
Better than what was coming.

Three nights later, the first machine arrived in Cogsbin.
Not a rumor.
Not a story carried by travelers.
A reality.
The city gathered in the central square to witness it.
Humans filled balconies.
Fae lined rooftops.
Merchants abandoned their stalls.
Children climbed crates for a better view.
Excitement rippled through the crowd.
A procession entered from the eastern road.
Wagons rolled forward carrying polished metal components.
Workers shouted instructions.
Officials smiled broadly.
At the center of everything stood the machine.
It towered above the crowd.
Massive.
Bronze.
Covered in pipes and gears.
A towering iron chimney rose from its center.
People stared in wonder.
Some applauded.
Others simply gawked.
Dagda and Morrigan watched from the edge of the square.
The shadows pooled uneasily around their feet.
The machine disturbed them.
Not because of its appearance.
Because of what it felt like.
Morrigan narrowed her eyes.
"Do you feel that?"
Dagda nodded immediately.
The sensation was unmistakable.
Essence.
Weak.
Subtle.
Yet present.
Like perfume lingering in a room after someone had departed.
The machine carried traces of it.
Tiny traces.
But enough.
Enough to confirm their fears.
A man climbed onto a platform beside the device.
Rich clothing.
Expensive jewelry.
A public figure.
One of the city's industrial investors.
His smile gleamed beneath sunlight.
"My friends!"
Cheers erupted.
The man spread his arms dramatically.
"Today marks the beginning of a new age!"
More applause.
More excitement.
The crowd leaned forward.
Hungry for possibility.
Hungry for progress.
The man gestured toward the machine.
"No longer shall industry depend upon magical labor."
The statement struck like a blade.
Dagda felt Morrigan tense beside him.
The crowd, however, cheered.
Most humans saw nothing wrong with the words.
Why would they?
To them it sounded liberating.
Promising.
Modern.
The speaker continued.
"Steam will carry us forward!"
Thunderous applause followed.
The machine roared to life.
Metal groaned.
Pipes hissed.
White vapor erupted from vents.
The crowd gasped.
Children pointed excitedly.
Merchants smiled.
Workers celebrated.
Only the Fae remained silent.
Not all.
But enough.
Because many of them felt it too.
That faint trace beneath the machine's operation.
That whisper hidden beneath the roar.
Essence.
Consumed.
Burned.
Used.
Morrigan's stomach twisted.
The lie stood before everyone.
Visible.
Celebrated.
Applauded.
And no one saw it.

The Shadow People gathered that evening.
The meeting hall filled beyond capacity.
Fear had spread.
Not panic.
Not yet.
Concern.
Questions.
Suspicion.
Dagda stood near the front.
Morrigan beside him.
Faces turned toward them.
Waiting.
The room felt tense.
Heavy.
Everyone understood that something fundamental had changed.
One of the older Akasha-touched Fae spoke first.
"What do we do?"
No one answered immediately.
Because no one knew.
Another voice emerged.
"They are lying."
Agreement rippled through the room.
The statement required no explanation.
Everyone knew what it meant.
Steam.
The machine.
The traces of Essence.
The deception.
"They won't stop," someone whispered.
The room fell silent.
Those words frightened people more than anything else.
Because deep down they all sensed the same truth.
This was only the beginning.
Morrigan stepped forward.
The shadows along the walls stirred.
Listening.
Watching.
"We gather evidence."
A murmur moved through the hall.
"We learn how they are doing it."
Another pause.
"We do not strike first."
Several faces showed disappointment.
Others relief.
Dagda understood both reactions.
Fear often created two impulses.
Fight.
Or hide.
Neither solved anything alone.
The room slowly settled.
Not because anyone felt reassured.
Because they trusted her.
Because they trusted him.
Sometimes trust was enough to postpone panic.
For a little while.

The evidence arrived sooner than expected.
A Vayu Fae named Aedan stumbled into Cogsbin six days later.
Half-dead.
Bloodied.
Terrified.
Dagda found him first.
The young Fae collapsed near the bakery shortly before dawn.
By the time Morrigan reached them, Eamon had already helped carry him upstairs.
The apartment felt crowded.
Small.
The wounded Fae lay upon their bed.
His skin had turned pale.
Dangerously pale.
Morrigan knelt beside him.
His breathing sounded shallow.
Weak.
Wrong.
"What happened?"
Aedan's eyes fluttered open.
Recognition appeared.
Then fear.
Raw fear.
The kind no one could fake.
"They took them."
Dagda leaned closer.
"Who?"
The answer emerged between ragged breaths.
"Humans."
Silence.
Aedan swallowed painfully.
"There were cages."
Morrigan felt ice settle inside her chest.
The room seemed colder.
Smaller.
The young Fae continued.
"They said it was for industry."
Tears appeared.
"They drained them."
No one spoke.
Because they already knew.
Yet hearing it aloud made everything worse.
Aedan's voice broke.
"There were children."
The words shattered something inside Morrigan.
Not visibly.
Not outwardly.
But she felt it.
A crack.
A wound.
A grief not entirely her own.
The shadows darkened around the room.
Reacting.
Responding.
Listening.
Dagda's hands clenched.
His jaw tightened.
For several moments he said nothing.
Then quietly—
"Where?"
Aedan named a location outside Steelmills.
A processing facility.
An industrial compound.
A place hidden behind walls.
A place no one had investigated thoroughly.
Until now.
________________________________________
Three days later Dagda returned.
Alone.
He entered the apartment after midnight.
Covered in dirt.
Exhausted.
Silent.
Morrigan knew immediately.
He had found it.
The answer existed in his eyes before he spoke.
She rose from her chair.
The fire crackled softly nearby.
Outside, rain had finally returned.
Dagda removed his cloak.
His hands trembled.
Not from fear.
From fury.
Morrigan approached slowly.
"What did you see?"
He stared at the floor.
For several seconds he could not answer.
The memories remained too fresh.
Too vivid.
Finally—
"Cages."
The word emerged like poison.
Morrigan closed her eyes.
Dagda continued.
"They drain Essence."
His voice sounded distant.
Hollow.
"They collect it."
Rain tapped softly against the window.
Neither moved.
Neither interrupted.
"They refine it."
A long silence followed.
Then—
"They call it steam."
Morrigan felt physically ill.
Because there it was.
The entire truth.
Simple.
Hideous.
Real.
Steam.
The word itself had become camouflage.
A lie wrapped around another lie.
A story designed to make exploitation sound like progress.
Dagda sat heavily beside the fire.
The flames reflected in his eyes.
For a moment he looked older.
Not physically.
Spiritually.
As though something precious had been taken from him.
Morrigan sat beside him.
Close enough for their shoulders to touch.
Neither spoke.
The silence lasted a long time.
Eventually Dagda whispered—
"I couldn't save them."
The confession hurt more than anything else.
Because Morrigan knew him.
Knew how deeply he carried responsibility.
Knew how impossible that burden had become.
She reached for his hand.
Held it tightly.
"You went."
His eyes remained fixed on the fire.
"It wasn't enough."
"No."
The answer came gently.
Honest.
Painful.
"No, it wasn't."
Dagda finally looked at her.
Grief lingered there.
Helplessness.
Anger.
Everything he rarely allowed others to see.
Morrigan touched his face.
"You cannot save everyone."
His expression tightened.
"Then what am I supposed to do?"
The question emerged raw.
Unfiltered.
Human.
For a moment neither leader nor legend existed.
Only a man watching his world become something monstrous.
Morrigan's heart broke for him.
Because she had no answer.
Only presence.
Only love.
Only the promise that he would not carry it alone.
She rested her forehead against his.
The shadows around them settled.
Quiet.
Protective.
Family.
And for a little while they simply sat together listening to the rain.
________________________________________
The following morning new posters appeared throughout Cogsbin.
Humans gathered around them curiously.
Workers discussed them openly.
Merchants approved.
Officials smiled.
The notices carried government seals.
Formal language.
Public declarations.
New regulations.
New protections.
New requirements.
Fae citizens were ordered to register.
For safety.
For accountability.
For prosperity.
The wording sounded harmless.
Reasonable.
Helpful.
That was precisely what made it dangerous.
Morrigan read one posted near the square.
Her expression hardened.
Dagda stood beside her.
Neither needed to speak.
The message was clear.
Registration always preceded control.
Control always preceded abuse.
History repeated itself with remarkable consistency.
Around them humans continued walking.
Talking.
Living.
Many saw nothing troubling.
Many genuinely believed the laws would help.
That hurt almost as much as deliberate cruelty.
Because ignorance could become a weapon just as easily as malice.
Morrigan stared at the poster.
The paper fluttered gently in the wind.
The first legal chain had appeared.
Thin.
Polished.
Almost invisible.
Yet a chain nonetheless.
And somewhere deep inside her, the shadows stirred.
Not fear.
Not anger.
Recognition.
The world had crossed a threshold.
The lie called steam had become law.
And laws, once born, rarely remained small.
