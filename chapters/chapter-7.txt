Morrigan felt the movement again before dawn.
She woke suddenly.
Not from fear.
Not from discomfort.
From awareness.
A strange certainty.
For several moments she remained still beneath the blankets, staring into darkness.
The apartment was quiet.
The city slept.
Only the distant creaking of moored airships drifted through the window.
Slowly she placed a hand against her stomach.
Waited.
Nothing.
Then—
A flutter.
Tiny.
Gentle.
Gone almost immediately.
Morrigan inhaled sharply.
The sensation was unlike anything she had ever experienced.
Not pain.
Not pressure.
Presence.
Life announcing itself.
Small enough to vanish in an instant.
Powerful enough to change everything.
Tears gathered unexpectedly.
She laughed softly to herself.
Then felt ridiculous for laughing.
Then laughed again.
Beside her, Dagda stirred.
His eyes opened.
Sleepy confusion appeared first.
Recognition followed.
Then concern.
"Morrigan?"
She shook her head.
"No."
His concern deepened immediately.
"No?"
A smile broke across her face.
The kind he had not seen in weeks.
"Not no."
Dagda blinked.
Clearly lost.
Morrigan grabbed his hand.
Pressed it gently against her stomach.
For several moments nothing happened.
Dagda waited.
Patient.
Confused.
Then suddenly—
The flutter returned.
His eyes widened.
The reaction happened instantly.
Pure disbelief.
Pure wonder.
Pure joy.
Neither spoke.
Neither needed to.
Because the expression on his face said everything.
Morrigan would remember it for the rest of her life.
The moment their child became real.
Not an idea.
Not a future.
A person.
A tiny life reaching outward from the darkness.
Dagda swallowed hard.
The movement came again.
Slightly stronger.
His breath caught.
Morrigan watched tears gather in his eyes.
He laughed once.
A short, helpless sound.
Then covered his face.
Overwhelmed.
Completely overwhelmed.
Morrigan smiled through tears of her own.
Outside, dawn slowly touched the rooftops of Cogsbin.
Inside, a family quietly became three.
________________________________________
The joy lasted four days.
Not because it ended.
Because reality returned.
A Shadow Person named Eira arrived shortly after sunset on the fourth evening.
She barely reached the bakery before collapsing.
Her cloak was torn.
Mud stained her clothing.
Blood covered one sleeve.
Not her blood.
The moment Dagda saw her expression, he knew.
Bad news.
The worst kind.
Eira sat at the kitchen table while Morrigan prepared tea.
Her hands trembled visibly.
The room remained silent until the cup rested before her.
Only then did she speak.
"They found another facility."
Dagda lowered himself into a chair.
"Where?"
"Outside Crudsder."
Silence followed.
Crudsder.
The smallest city in Eidolon.
Remote.
Wooded.
Far from the industrial expansion consuming Cogsbin and Steelmills.
The location surprised everyone.
Eira stared at the untouched tea.
"They aren't hiding anymore."
The statement hung heavily.
Because it felt true.
The secrecy was fading.
Confidence was replacing caution.
The people responsible no longer seemed afraid of discovery.
Why would they be?
The laws increasingly favored them.
The newspapers protected them.
The public believed them.
Power had a way of creating boldness.
Especially among those who should never possess it.
"What did you see?" Morrigan asked quietly.
Eira's jaw tightened.
"Cages."
The word again.
Always the same word.
Always the same horror.
The young woman swallowed.
"There were dozens."
Dagda closed his eyes briefly.
Eira continued.
"They're separating the stronger Essence types."
Silence.
Morrigan felt cold.
The room seemed to shrink around her.
"What do you mean?"
Eira finally looked up.
Fear lingered there.
Raw.
Visible.
"They know the difference now."
No one spoke.
Because everyone understood.
Humans had learned.
Not everything.
But enough.
Enough to categorize.
Enough to identify.
Enough to harvest more efficiently.
The realization sickened Morrigan.
The child inside her shifted slightly.
A tiny movement.
A reminder.
A reason.
A responsibility.
________________________________________
The following morning Dagda called a meeting.
Not merely for the Shadow People.
For any Fae willing to come.
The hall overflowed.
Seelie Fae.
UnSeelie Fae.
Akasha-dominant.
Prithvi-dominant.
Jala- dominant.
Vayu- dominant.
Agni- dominant.
Differences suddenly mattered less than survival.
Faces filled every available space.
Fear connected them.
Dagda stood before the gathering.
The room quieted.
Not instantly.
Gradually.
Like a storm settling.
When silence finally arrived, he spoke.
"The disappearances are not random."
No one reacted.
Most already knew.
"The facilities are expanding."
Murmurs spread.
Anxious.
Uneasy.
Angry.
Dagda allowed them a moment.
Then continued.
"We have spent months investigating."
His gaze moved across the room.
Meeting eyes.
Sharing truth.
"We now know what they are doing."
The hall grew still.
Very still.
"They are harvesting Essence."
The words landed heavily.
Even those who already suspected seemed shaken hearing it spoken aloud.
Because suspicions remained softer than certainty.
Certainty possesses weight.
The room erupted.
Voices rose immediately.
Questions.
Arguments.
Fear.
Dagda did not attempt to stop them.
The emotions needed release.
Needed acknowledgment.
Eventually Morrigan stepped forward.
The shadows along the walls stirred.
Responding instinctively.
The sight quieted the room.
Not through intimidation.
But recognition.
Respect.
The Shadow Faction trusted her.
All Fae Respected her.
So, all Fae listened.
"We have a choice."
Silence settled again.
"We can continue pretending this ends on its own."
No one moved.
No one spoke.
Morrigan's voice remained calm.
Measured.
Steady.
"Or we can prepare."
The word lingered.
Prepare.
Not fight.
Not retaliate.
Prepare.
The distinction mattered.
Because she already knew.
War had begun.
Only one side had noticed.
________________________________________
The preparations transformed daily life.
Safe routes were established.
Messages carried secretly.
Families relocated.
Hidden shelters expanded.
The Shadow People worked tirelessly.
So did many others.
For the first time, old rivalries weakened.
Necessity accomplished what diplomacy never could.
Fear united people.
At least temporarily.
Yet beneath the cooperation, another reality lingered.
Not everyone would leave.
Not everyone believed the danger.
Not everyone wanted to abandon homes built across generations.
Dagda understood them.
That made it worse.
Because he understood exactly why they hesitated.
Every road led away from something precious.
Every escape required sacrifice.
Every act of survival demanded loss.
________________________________________
One evening Morrigan walked alone through the city.
She had not intended to.
She simply needed air.
Needed movement.
Needed a moment without responsibility pressing against her shoulders.
Autumn had fully arrived.
Leaves drifted through narrow streets.
Lanterns glowed warmly from windows.
The city looked beautiful.
Painfully beautiful.
Morrigan passed familiar places.
The textile shop.
The market square.
The bookstore she and Dagda visited during quieter years.
The fountain where children played during summer afternoons.
Every corner held memories.
Every street carried meaning.
Home.
The word hurt now.
Because home was becoming something she would soon lose.
She eventually found herself near the docks.
Airships swayed gently against their moorings.
Ropes creaked softly.
The scent of salt lingered in the wind.
Morrigan leaned against a railing overlooking dark water.
The harbor reflected scattered lanternlight.
Gold upon black.
Beautiful.
Fragile.
Temporary.
A voice spoke behind her.
"You look sad."
Morrigan turned.
Eamon stood nearby holding a wrapped loaf of bread.
She smiled faintly.
"You look nosy."
The baker snorted.
"That's because I am."
Despite herself, she laughed.
Eamon approached.
Rested his arms upon the railing.
For a while they simply watched the harbor.
Neither spoke.
Eventually the baker sighed.
"You're leaving."
It wasn't a question.
Morrigan stared at the water.
"Maybe."
Eamon grunted.
"Terrible liar."
The answer almost made her cry.
Because she realized he already knew.
Perhaps everyone knew.
The city itself seemed to know.
The ending had arrived.
The baker handed her the loaf.
Warm.
Fresh.
Comforting.
Morrigan accepted it.
Confused.
"Eamon—"
"For the road."
The simplicity of the statement struck harder than any farewell.
For several seconds she could not speak.
The baker avoided her eyes.
Studied the harbor instead.
"You don't have to explain anything."
The words emerged quietly.
Gentle.
"I know enough."
Morrigan swallowed hard.
Emotion tightened her throat.
Eamon finally looked at her.
Old eyes.
Kind eyes.
Human eyes.
The eyes of a friend.
"Take care of yourself."
A pause.
Then another.
"And take care of her."
Morrigan froze.
The baker smiled.
Small.
Knowing.
"You're not nearly as subtle as you think."
For the first time in weeks, genuine happiness broke through her grief.
She laughed.
Eamon laughed too.
The harbor echoed softly with the sound.
A final memory.
A final gift.
Neither realized how much it would matter later.
________________________________________
That night Morrigan returned home carrying the loaf.
Dagda immediately noticed her expression.
"You've been crying."
"I have not."
"You absolutely have."
Morrigan rolled her eyes.
Dagda pointed.
"Evidence."
She set the bread upon the table.
"You're becoming insufferable."
His smile widened.
"Only becoming?"
The answer finally made her laugh.
A real laugh.
Bright.
Warm.
Brief.
The apartment felt alive again for a moment.
Like the years before fear arrived.
Like the years before… steam.
Like years before the world began breaking.
Later, after the laughter faded and silence returned, they sat together beside the window.
The city glowed beyond the glass.
Familiar.
Beloved.
Dying.
Neither said the words aloud.
Neither needed to.
Because both understood.
Their time in Cogsbin was ending.
The city that had given them peace could no longer protect them.
The child growing within Morrigan had made that impossible.
And somewhere beyond the city walls, beyond the roads and forests of Eidolon, hunters were already moving.
Searching.
Learning.
Following trails.
Following rumors.
Following whispers of a powerful pregnant Fae whose Essence carried something unlike anything they had ever encountered.
The shadows felt them coming long before anyone else did.
They gathered thickly around the apartment that night.
Watching.
Listening.
Waiting.
And for the first time since arriving in Cogsbin years ago, Dagda barred the door before sleeping.
The gesture was small.
Simple.
Ordinary.
Yet both of them understood what it meant.
Home was no longer safe.
