The knock came shortly before sunrise.
Not loud.
Not violent.
Three measured strikes against the bakery door below.
Then silence.
Dagda woke instantly.
Years of caution had trained him well.
Beside him, Morrigan remained asleep.
One hand rested lightly upon her stomach.
For a brief moment he simply watched her.
The pale gray light filtering through the curtains.
The rise and fall of her breathing.
The peaceful expression she only wore while sleeping.
Then the knock came again.
Three more strikes.
Patient.
Waiting.
Dagda carefully slipped from bed.
The floorboards creaked softly beneath his feet.
Below, he heard movement.
Eamon.
Now - the bakery owner - was already awake.
Already answering the door.
The conversation that followed sounded hushed.
Urgent.
Dagda did not like that.
He moved toward the apartment door.
Then paused.
The shadows had already gathered.
Dark pools stretching along corners and walls.
Listening.
Alert.
Something was wrong.
Very wrong.
________________________________________
The visitor was a Shadow Fae named Rowan.
Young.
Quick-footed.
Usually composed.
This morning he looked terrified.
Rain soaked his cloak.
Mud stained his boots.
His face had lost all color.
Eamon stood nearby holding a lantern.
The old baker's expression mirrored the young Fae's concern.
The moment Dagda descended the stairs, Rowan looked up.
Relief flashed across his face.
Then urgency returned.
"They know."
Silence filled the bakery.
The ovens had not yet begun warming.
The room felt cold.
Still.
Dagda's stomach tightened.
"What do they know?"
Rowan swallowed.
"Madame’s pregnancy."
The answer struck like a blade.
For several seconds no one moved.
No one spoke.
The rain outside seemed louder suddenly.
Heavier.
Closer.
Dagda stared at the young fae.
Certain he had misheard.
Certain he wanted to have misheard.
But Rowan's face held only truth.
Only fear.
Only certainty.
"How?"
The question emerged quietly.
Dangerously quiet.
Rowan shook his head.
"I…I don't know."
A pause.
"I only heard about it this morning…and I only know the information is spreading."
The shadows along the walls darkened.
Listening.
Waiting.
Dagda felt cold anger settle inside him.
Not panic.
Not fear.
Something sharper.
More focused.
Because rumors could be managed.
Rumors could be denied.
But information spreading among humans hunting Fae?
That was different.
That was deadly.
________________________________________
Morrigan heard everything from the stairs.
She had awakened when Dagda left.
By the time she reached the lower floor, the conversation was nearly finished.
The moment she saw Rowan's face, she understood.
Bad news.
The worst kind.
The young Fae repeated everything.
Slowly.
Carefully.
No details improved the situation.
Someone had spoken.
Someone had seen.
Someone had shared information.
No one knew who.
No one knew where.
Only that descriptions of Morrigan and Dagda now existed.
Descriptions of powerful Fae.
Descriptions of unusual shadow manifestations.
Descriptions of Morrigan’s pregnancy.
They knew what she looked like.
This was the kind of information that traveled quickly.
The kind of information that attracted attention.
Dangerous attention.
Morrigan listened silently.
When Rowan finally finished, the bakery remained quiet.
Even Eamon said nothing.
Because there was nothing to say.
The conclusion stood obvious before everyone.
Cogsbin was finished.
The city could no longer protect them.
________________________________________
By noon the decision had been made.
Not publicly.
Not ceremonially.
Simply accepted.
They would leave.
Immediately.
The realization felt surreal.
Years of life compressed into a handful of hours.
Everything they owned suddenly became categories.
Necessary.
Unnecessary.
Leave behind.
Take.
The apartment was transformed into organized chaos.
Books disappeared into bags.
Clothing folded.
Supplies were gathered.
Maps retrieved.
The work felt mechanical.
Unreal.
Morrigan hated every moment.
Not because of the labor.
Because every object carried memory.
A cup purchased during their first year in the city.
A blanket gifted by neighbors.
Books read beside winter fires.
Tiny pieces of a life built patiently over time.
Now reduced to choices.
Keep.
Leave.
Keep.
Leave.
Each decision felt like grief.
________________________________________
Dagda found her standing motionless beside the window.
The packing had stopped.
A half-filled satchel of herbs rested nearby.
Outside, rain coated the city in silver.
The streets shimmered.
Beautiful.
Familiar.
Home.
Morrigan didn't look at him when he approached.
"I thought we'd have more time."
The words emerged softly.
Dagda stood beside her.
For a while neither spoke.
The city beyond the glass continued moving.
Merchants opened shops.
Children hurried through puddles.
Ordinary life.
Unaware.
Unchanged.
At least on the surface.
"We had years."
Morrigan smiled sadly.
"That isn't enough."
"No."
The answer came immediately.
Because nothing would have been enough.
Not for this.
Not for the life they built here.
The silence stretched.
Heavy.
Comfortable.
Painful.
Then Morrigan laughed unexpectedly.
A single breath of laughter.
Dagda glanced toward her.
"What?"
She pointed toward the street below.
Eamon stood outside the bakery arguing with a delivery driver.
The baker gestured dramatically.
The driver appeared equally offended.
Neither seemed willing to surrender.
The scene would have been amusing under any circumstance.
Today it felt precious.
Normal.
Mortal.
Alive.
Morrigan watched for another moment.
Then whispered—
"I'm going to miss this place."
Dagda took her hand.
"So will I."
________________________________________
The farewell began before they intended it to.
Word spread quickly.
People noticed activity.
Packed supplies.
Unusual movement.
By afternoon visitors began arriving.
Some were members of the Shadow Fae Faction.
Others weren't.
Neighbors.
Friends.
People whose lives had intersected with theirs over the years.
Some were human.
Most were Fae.
No speeches occurred.
No dramatic declarations.
Simply visits.
Conversations.
Moments.
A mortal woman delivered preserves.
Another brought handmade blankets.
A Fae craftsman repaired damaged travel gear without even being asked.
Children appeared carrying flowers.
The gestures accumulated.
Small acts of affection.
Small acts of gratitude.
Small acts of love.
The Good People rarely express until departure forces honesty.
Morrigan accepted each gift with growing difficulty.
Because every kindness made leaving harder, not easier.
________________________________________
That evening Eamon closed the bakery early.
No explanation given.
None needed.
The old man carried several loaves upstairs.
Along with cheese, fruit, and a bottle of cider.
The moment Morrigan saw everything, she frowned.
"Eamon."
The baker ignored her.
"Eamon."
Still ignored.
"Eamon."
Finally, he sighed.
"What?"
"We can't possibly eat all this."
"You underestimate yourselves."
Dagda almost smiled.
Almost.
The baker arranged the food across the table.
Then sat heavily in a chair.
Silence followed.
Comfortable silence.
The kind that is shared only among those who no longer required conversation to prove affection.
Eventually Eamon cleared his throat.
"You know."
Everyone looked toward him.
The baker stared at the tabletop.
"If circumstances were different..."
His voice faltered briefly.
Then steadied.
"…I'd tell you to stay."
Morrigan's chest tightened.
No one spoke.
Because everyone understood.
Circumstances were not different.
Reality remained reality.
The old baker exhaled slowly.
"But they aren't."
A pause.
"So, I'm not going to insult you by pretending."
That honesty hurt, yet it also comforted.
Because false hope would have been cruel.
The truth, at least, respected them.
Eamon looked toward Morrigan.
Then Dagda.
Then the packed bags near the wall.
"You leave tomorrow."
Neither corrected him.
Because he was right.
The decision had already been made.
The road was waiting.
The future was waiting.
The danger was waiting.
The old man nodded once.
As though confirming something to himself.
Then he reached for the cider.
"Then eat."
The command sounded suspiciously emotional.
Morrigan smiled.
Dagda did too.
And for a little while they shared a meal together.
Not because it resolved anything.
Because this memory mattered.
Because some moments deserved preservation.
Even when endings approached.
Especially when endings approach.
________________________________________
The rain intensified overnight.
Wind rattled windows.
Thunder rolled across distant hills.
The city slept beneath storm clouds.
Dagda did not.
He stood alone beside the apartment window.
Watching.
Thinking.
The shadows drifted quietly around the room.
Restless.
Alert.
Several times he thought he saw movement below.
Figures lingering too long.
Watching the bakery.
Watching the street.
Watching.
Perhaps it was his imagination.
Perhaps not.
The distinction mattered less than it once had.
Because caution was now survival.
Eventually Morrigan joined him.
She wrapped a blanket around her shoulders.
Leaned gently against him.
Neither spoke.
The storm filled the silence.
After several minutes she whispered—
"Do you think we'll ever come back?"
The question lingered.
Heavy.
Impossible.
Dagda looked out across Cogsbin.
The rooftops.
The lanterns.
The streets.
The city that had become their home.
Then he looked toward Morrigan.
Toward the child she carried, in her swollen belly.
Toward the future.
And he knew the truth.
Even if they returned someday, it would never be this place again.
Too much had changed.
Too much was changing.
The world was moving toward something new.
Something darker.
Something hungry.
Eventually he answered.
"I don't know."
Morrigan nodded.
Honesty was enough.
Neither needed promises.
Neither needed lies.
Only truth.
Only each other.
Only the strength to keep moving.
Below them, rain hammered the streets of Cogsbin.
Above them, thunder rolled across the heavens.
And somewhere beyond the city, beyond the roads and forests of Eidolon, men were already preparing cages.
Already preparing wagons.
Already preparing chains.
Hunters moving through darkness.
Following rumors.
Following descriptions.
Following whispers of a pregnant Fae whose blood might increase their progression.
The shadows felt them drawing nearer.
Closer, and closer still.
By dawn, leaving would no longer be a choice.
It would become survival.
