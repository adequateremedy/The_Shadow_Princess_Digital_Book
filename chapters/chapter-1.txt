Long before anyone called them the Shadow People, they were simply Fae.
They walked beneath the same skies as everyone else.
They laughed in market squares. They traded favors for bread and stories. They crossed bridges beside humans who scarcely noticed the subtle glow of Essence moving beneath their skin. Children pointed at bright glamour drifting from fingertips and thought it beautiful rather than frightening.
The world had not yet learned suspicion.
In those days, darkness was merely the absence of light.
No one had yet discovered it could become something else.
No one except a handful of Fae whose blood carried an anomaly that neither Court understood.
The first whispers began in the depths of the UnSeelie lands.
They spoke of Fae who stepped into darkness and emerged carrying pieces of it with them.
Not illusions.
Not glamour.
Not tricks of perception.
Something older.
Something that lingered.
The shadows followed them.
The shadows listened.
The shadows remembered.
Many feared it.
Others worshiped it.
Most simply watched.
Dagda watched.
He remembered the first time he saw it.
The memory remained sharper than any blade.
A cold evening.
Rain striking black stone.
Torchlight trembling against the walls of a gathering hall.
The UnSeelie nobles had assembled to discuss the collapse of yet another faction. Politics. Accusations. Endless grievances disguised as civility.
Dagda had hated every moment of it.
The room smelled of damp wool and extinguished candles.
Voices rose and fell like distant storms.
Then silence arrived.
A woman had entered.
Morrigan.
She was not yet the figure history would remember.
Not yet feared.
Not yet revered.
Simply Morrigan.
Her dark hair hung damp from the rain.
Her expression carried neither arrogance nor submission.
But something unusual moved behind her.
Not beside her.
Not beneath her.
Behind her.
The shadows themselves seemed reluctant to leave.
They stretched toward her footsteps.
Clung to her outline.
Watched the room through her eyes.
A murmur swept through the hall.
Dagda felt it too.
Not fear.
Recognition.
Something inside his Essence stirred.
The sensation unsettled him.
As though a locked door had opened somewhere deep within his soul.
Morrigan crossed the chamber.
Ignored the staring faces.
Ignored the whispers.
Ignored Queen Mab herself.
She stopped beside an empty window.
Looked out at the rain.
And smiled.
The shadows smiled with her.
That was the moment Dagda understood.
Whatever she carried, he carried it too.
He simply had not known its name.
________________________________________
Their first conversation happened three nights later.
The rain had returned.
Dagda found her standing alone beside a cliff overlooking black waters.
Wind pulled at her cloak.
Below, waves crashed against stone.
Morrigan did not turn when he approached.
“You've been following me.”
Dagda folded his arms.
“You noticed.”
“I notice everything.”
The answer should have sounded arrogant.
Instead it sounded tired.
She finally glanced toward him.
Silver eyes.
Ancient eyes.
Eyes that seemed perpetually occupied by thoughts no one else could hear.
“You have it too,” she said.
Dagda froze.
“The shadows.”
The wind howled between them.
For several moments neither spoke.
Then Dagda stepped closer.
“How long have you known?”
“Since childhood.”
“You were born with it?”
“Yes.”
Something tightened in his chest.
He had spent years believing he was broken.
Years hiding abilities that no tutor could explain.
Years pretending the darkness obeying him was ordinary magic.
Now another stood before him carrying the same burden.
The same mystery.
The same isolation.
Morrigan looked back toward the sea.
“They fear it.”
“Do you?”
“No.”
Her answer came immediately.
Without hesitation.
Without doubt.
“No,” she repeated. “I fear what happens when people decide they understand something they do not.”
Dagda found himself smiling.
It was the first genuine smile he had offered in months.
Morrigan noticed.
For a brief moment she smiled too.
And the shadows around them softened.
As though relieved.
________________________________________
Their friendship grew quietly.
Neither intended it.
Neither resisted it.
Days became conversations.
Conversations became habits.
Habits became necessity.
They wandered forests together.
Studied strange manifestations of darkness together.
Tested limits.
Learned.
Failed.
Learned again.
The shadows behaved differently around them.
Stronger.
More responsive.
More alive.
They discovered they could step between pockets of darkness.
Hear echoes lingering in abandoned places.
Feel the emotional residue left behind by powerful moments.
Not memories.
Not visions.
Something stranger.
Liminal impressions.
Spaces between certainty and forgetting.
The deeper they explored, the more isolated they became.
The Courts disliked unexplained things.
The Seelie distrusted them openly.
The UnSeelie admired their usefulness while quietly fearing their independence.
Neither response mattered.
Dagda cared only about the woman walking beside him.
And Morrigan increasingly felt the same.
Though neither admitted it.
Not at first.
________________________________________
Winter arrived.
Snow blanketed the hills.
Ice coated the trees.
One evening they sheltered inside an abandoned watchtower overlooking frozen fields.
A small fire crackled between them.
Orange light danced across stone walls.
Outside, wind screamed through broken windows.
Inside, warmth gathered.
Morrigan sat wrapped in a dark blanket.
Dagda watched flames.
Neither spoke for some time.
Comfortable silence.
Rare silence.
The kind that existed only between people who understood one another.
Finally Morrigan sighed.
“Do you ever wonder what it would feel like?”
Dagda glanced toward her.
“What?”
“To belong somewhere.”
The question struck harder than expected.
Morrigan stared into the fire.
“I am tired of being observed.”
Her voice softened.
“Tired of being useful.”
Another pause.
“Tired of being feared.”
Dagda looked at her.
Really looked.
Not the powerful Fae.
Not the strange anomaly.
Not the growing legend.
Just Morrigan.
Lonely.
Exhausted.
Human in all the ways that mattered.
“You belong with me.”
The words escaped before he could stop them.
Silence followed.
Firelight flickered.
Snow rattled against stone.
Morrigan slowly raised her eyes.
Neither looked away.
Neither laughed.
Neither pretended the words meant less than they did.
Because they both knew.
The truth had been growing between them for months.
Perhaps longer.
Morrigan reached across the space separating them.
Her fingers touched his.
The shadows gathered around the fire.
Listening.
Watching.
Witnessing.
“I know,” she whispered.
And the world changed.
________________________________________
The years that followed became the foundation of every choice they would ever make.
They built a life together.
Not a grand one.
Not a royal one.
Simply theirs.
The Courts continued their games.
Politics continued.
Power shifted endlessly between ambitious hands.
Dagda and Morrigan remained outside most of it.
The anomaly within their blood refused to stay hidden.
Others began appearing.
Rare.
Scattered.
Different.
Fae carrying traces of Akasha Essence.
Fae connected to shadow in ways previously thought impossible.
Some sought guidance.
Others sought understanding.
Many sought refuge.
Dagda welcomed them.
Morrigan listened to them.
The gatherings grew.
Slowly.
Quietly.
Unintentionally.
What began as conversations became community.
What became community evolved into identity.
The Midnight Vanguard emerged from the remnants of a fallen faction.
Not because someone declared it.
Because people needed somewhere to stand.
Needed someone to understand them.
Needed somewhere they could stop apologizing for existing.
The shadows followed.
Always.
Watching from corners.
Listening from thresholds.
Living within the liminal spaces others ignored.
The name came from outsiders.
Not from themselves.
The Shadow People.
At first it was mockery.
An insult.
A warning.
Look away.
Stay clear.
Do not trust them.
Yet the name endured.
Because it contained truth.
They worked in shadows.
Protected from shadows.
Learned from shadows.
They became caretakers of forgotten places.
Guardians of thresholds.
Witnesses to what others refused to see.
Eventually they embraced the name.
Not as surrender.
As ownership.
________________________________________
The friendship between Morrigan and Queen Mab deteriorated gradually.
Painfully.
Like a rope fraying strand by strand.
There had once been genuine affection between them.
Mutual trust.
Mutual respect.
Those days faded.
Mab viewed the growing faction with increasing concern.
Morrigan viewed the Court's indifference with growing disappointment.
Arguments replaced conversations.
Distance replaced trust.
By the time their final meeting arrived, neither recognized the relationship they once possessed.
The confrontation occurred beneath black banners inside an UnSeelie hall.
Dagda stood beside Morrigan.
Mab sat upon her throne.
Neither woman smiled.
“You are encouraging independence.”
Mab's voice echoed across stone.
“We are encouraging survival,” Morrigan replied.
“You gather loyalty.”
“We gather people.”
“They are not the same.”
Morrigan met her gaze.
“Sometimes they are.”
Silence followed.
Heavy.
Dangerous.
The distance between them suddenly felt immeasurable.
Mab leaned forward.
“You are forgetting where you came from.”
“No.”
Morrigan's voice remained calm.
“I remember perfectly.”
The answer ended everything.
Not with violence.
Not with threats.
With certainty.
The certainty that neither would bend.
The certainty that friendship had become history.
________________________________________
Several months later, Dagda and Morrigan departed.
Not exiled.
Not banished.
Simply gone.
The decision belonged entirely to them.
They chose Cogsbin.
A city in Eidolon.
A place where humans and Fae still lived side by side.
Where bakeries opened before dawn.
Where airships drifted overhead.
Where windmills turned lazily across distant hills.
Where ordinary life remained possible.
The journey took weeks.
They traveled with a handful of loyal followers.
Nothing grand.
Nothing ceremonial.
Just wagons.
Supplies.
Hope.
On the final evening before reaching the city, they camped beneath open stars.
A fire burned softly.
Most slept.
Dagda and Morrigan remained awake.
Watching embers drift skyward.
Listening to crickets sing in the darkness.
“You regret it?” Dagda asked.
Morrigan leaned against his shoulder.
“No.”
The answer came easily.
She watched the stars.
“For the first time in a long time, I do not know what comes next.”
Dagda smiled.
“That sounds terrifying.”
“It does.”
“Good.”
She laughed softly.
The sound warmed him more than the fire.
“Why good?”
“Because everything important in my life began with uncertainty.”
Morrigan turned toward him.
Moonlight touched her face.
Gentle.
Beautiful.
Familiar.
The shadows gathered quietly around their camp.
Patient.
Protective.
Home.
She reached for his hand.
Intertwined their fingers.
And for one perfect moment neither future nor destiny mattered.
Only the warmth between them.
Only the road ahead.
Only the city waiting beyond the horizon.
Neither knew darkness was already stirring beneath the surface of the world.
Neither knew treaties were beginning to crack.
Neither knew fear would soon become industry.
Neither knew blood would become fuel.
For now there was only peace.
For now there was only love.
For now there was only the long road leading toward Cogsbin.
